## 4.1 The cue-vs-script conflict, resolved by consumer and phase [MC §8.1]

Coaches say cue words only (a script = a teleprompter); Duarte/assertion-evidence practice needs full prose (notes become the handout; decks get handed off). Resolution for a generation pipeline — never average: **emit a full-sentence talk track written for the ear, headed by a cue skeleton.** A script can be compressed into cues by the presenter; cues can never be expanded into words the presenter didn't write; only full sentences allow handoff, timing math, and handout conversion. The notes preamble instructs the presenter to degrade to cues across rehearsals.

## 4.2 Per-slide notes template [MC §8.2]

1. **ONE-LINER** — the single spoken sentence that is the slide's point. Not the title read aloud (redundancy effect; the room reads faster than you speak) — same substance, spoken register. Deck test: the one-liners alone read as a coherent 60–90-second spoken version of the whole talk (the spoken twin of the title skim).
2. **TALK TRACK** — full sentences for the ear [speech consensus via MC]:
   - 8–16 words per sentence — listeners lose the thread beyond ~16–20 spoken words (vs ~30 written).
   - Contractions ("don't", not "do not") — but avoid "-'ve" forms that sound like "of" when heard.
   - Short common words: adults comprehend ~96% of speech with a ~2,000-word vocabulary; the ear has no re-read.
   - Verbs over nominalizations; first/second person allowed; rhetorical devices (anaphora, triads) live HERE, not on the canvas (body-microcopy §2.3).
   - The track carries the WHY and the story; the canvas shows the WHAT — the track never repeats canvas text (Mayer redundancy — message-redundant, wording-divergent; anti-patterns conflict 2).
3. **TRANSITION OUT** — the verbatim segue into the next slide. Openings, transitions, closings are the memorized-verbatim parts; transitions encode the argument's causal chain.
4. **[STAGE DIRECTIONS]** in brackets, syntactically separable: build cues (naming the narration beat they sync with — design §4.3), pauses, story markers, room prompts.
5. **BACKUP** — exact figures with sources (the reversibility home for translation-procedure §6.3 rounding), expected-question pointers to appendix slides, pronunciations. Quotes/statistics needing precise delivery are the sanctioned verbatim-reading exception.

## 4.3 Register: write aloud [MC §8.3]

Draft by transcribing how you'd say it to one person, then tighten; never by relaxing written prose — subordinate clauses and passives survive that path.

## 4.4 Notes are the timing instrument [MC §8.4]

Talk-track words ÷ 130 (dense/technical) to 150 (narrative) wpm ≈ minutes of dwell; compare against the slide's dwell budget (structure G9). An oversized track is a visible cut list before rehearsal. This check is automatable.

## 4.5 Handoff mode [MC §8.5; design §5.5]

For slidedoc/notes-page handouts, the talk track minus bracketed directions becomes the page prose — the strip is mechanical because directions are bracketed. Keep brackets syntactically separable for exactly this reason.

## 4.6 What routes to notes (the copy-side view of the three-artifact split)

The canvas rules of §1–§3 are only achievable because notes are the escape valve (design §5.5): sub-assertions, derivations, method detail, full citations, the exact figures behind rounded titles (§6.3 reversibility), jargon glosses that would inflate the headline (§6.2), long descriptions for complex exhibits (§3.6), and pre-answered objections with appendix pointers. Routing rule: anything the VERIFYING reader needs but the GLANCING reader doesn't goes to notes with a scent line — but honesty never depends on the routed layer (round-trip test, fact-fidelity-qa §7.1-5: the glanced layer alone must not overstate).
