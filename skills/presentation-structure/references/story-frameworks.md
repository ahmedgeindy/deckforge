# Story & Message Frameworks (A: message-level, C: story arcs)

> Reference file of the `presentation-structure` skill. Framework codes (A1, B2, C1...) are stable across SKILL.md and all reference files.

### A. Message-level frameworks (run before choosing any structure)

#### A1. The One-Sentence Message (TED Throughline / Duarte Big Idea)
- **Structure**: One sentence, ≤15 words, with an intriguing angle — an idea, not a topic ("More choice makes us less happy", not "choice"). Every element of the talk hangs on it; anything that doesn't attach gets cut.
- **When to use**: First step of every deck, before framework selection. Doubles as the kill-filter during outlining.
- **Slide mapping**: Not a sequence — a filter. Write it at the top of the outline; delete any slide that can't hang on it.
- **Pitfalls**: Writing a topic instead of an idea (no stake, no angle); overstuffing — Anderson's #1 diagnosed failure is covering a topic instead of driving one idea. Fix: cut range, add depth and examples.
- **Relatives**: Duarte's Big Idea (full sentence + stake) and Olson's one-sentence ABT are the same object. Minto's "governing thought" is its answer-first sibling.

#### A2. ABT — And, But, Therefore (Olson)
- **Structure**: "[Agreement] AND [context] BUT [one problem/contradiction] THEREFORE [resolution/action]." Diagnostic spectrum: AAA (and-and-and) = narratively dead report; DHY (despite-however-yet) = too many contradictions, confusion; ABT = sweet spot with exactly ONE "but".
- **When to use**: Distilling the narrative core before building; per-section and per-slide QC on any draft that feels like a list.
- **Slide mapping**: Works at three zoom levels: (a) whole deck — opening = AND (2–3 agreed-context slides), pivot = BUT (1–2 core-tension slides, the deck's hinge), remainder = THEREFORE; (b) per section — each body section internally runs A→B→T; (c) per title — consecutive action titles should alternate A/B/T, never stack three ANDs.
- **Pitfalls**: Experts default to AAA because expertise rewards completeness. More than one BUT per story unit = confusion. Place your most important evidence immediately AFTER the "but" — that's where attention spikes.
- **Relatives**: ABT is Duarte's gap and Minto's Complication compressed to sentence scale — which is why it composes with every other framework here.

### C. Story-arc family

#### C1. Duarte Sparkline (What-Is / What-Could-Be)
- **Structure**: (1) Beginning: establish "what is" — current reality the audience already agrees with (~10% of the talk). (2) Turning Point 1 "Call to Adventure": state the big idea, opening a stark GAP between what-is and what-could-be. (3) Middle: traverse repeatedly between what-is and what-could-be — contrast in content (current/potential), emotion (analytical/emotional), delivery (traditional/surprising). Embed ONE STAR moment. (4) Turning Point 2: Call to Action. (5) End "New Bliss": vivid picture of the world after adoption — end higher than you began.
- **When to use**: Persuading resistant audiences to abandon the status quo: launches, vision talks, change initiatives, keynotes. Beats answer-first when resistance is high; beats single-climax arcs for 20+ minute talks.
- **Slide mapping**: Slides 1–2: current reality in the audience's own words (they must nod). Slide 3: the gap — one bold "what could be" statement standing alone. Middle: 3–5 contrast PAIRS (each = 1–3 "today" slides then 1–3 "imagine" slides), one pair carrying the STAR slide; shorten the toggle interval near the end to build excitement. Penultimate: CTA (verb + owner + first step). Final: new bliss imagery — never logistics.
- **Pitfalls**: Spending too long in "what is" (Jobs spent most time in could-be, dipping down mainly to disparage rivals); flat middles with no genuine toggling; two STAR moments (they compete and cancel); ending on the CTA instead of the bliss.
- **Relatives**: The seed article's "Fact-and-Story Zig-Zag" IS the sparkline. Duarte derived it by modifying Freytag's pyramid (C5) and the Hero's Journey (C3). Each oscillation is a micro S-C pair (B2); "new bliss" = Monroe's Visualization step (C7) = the Answer delivered last for emotional payoff.

#### C2. Three-Act Structure
- **Structure**: Act 1 SETUP (~25%): audience-as-protagonist, antagonist force (challenge/competitor/status quo), inciting conflict, stakes. Act 2 CONFRONTATION (~50%): obstacles, escalation, data alternated with anecdote. Act 3 RESOLUTION (~25%): solution resolving the conflict, transformation, CTA.
- **When to use**: Default backbone for any 15+ minute talk; the scaffold under most other frameworks.
- **Slide mapping**: 10-slide deck: 1–3 setup (their world, the antagonist, stakes-as-question), 4–7 confrontation (evidence, roadblocks, why alternatives fail), 8–10 resolution (solution, proof, CTA). Place a visible turning-point slide at each act boundary.
- **Pitfalls**: THE dominant deck failure: "all Act 1" — endless context. Cap setup at 25%. Act 2 without genuine named opposition makes the resolution feel unearned.
- **Relatives**: Aristotle/Syd Field; the shape under the sparkline, Freytag, and the exec summary alike.

#### C3. Hero's Journey (audience-as-hero)
- **Structure**: Campbell/Vogler's 12 stages, condensed for talks to 5–6 beats: ordinary world → call → refusal (doubts) → mentor's gift → ordeal → return transformed. Adaptation rule: audience = hero, presenter = mentor ("you're Yoda, not Luke Skywalker").
- **When to use**: Founder/origin stories, personal narratives, change-management journeys. As a persuasion overlay: your content is the mentor's gift; the CTA is the audience's threshold-crossing.
- **Slide mapping**: Origin deck (8–10 slides): ordinary world → the event that forced change + honest doubts → one obstacle per slide incl. real failures → the darkest moment (lowest emotional point) → what was learned / the elixir → what it means for the audience. Persuasion overlay: map audience's state to Ordinary World, your idea to the Call, their objections to Refusal (voice them out loud), evidence to the Mentor's gift, CTA to Crossing the Threshold.
- **Pitfalls**: Presenter-as-hero — reads as self-congratulation. Marching through all 12 stages — bloat; 5–6 beats suffice. No opposition = no journey = no reason to act.
- **Relatives**: Raskin's sales narrative (D7) is the hero's journey with customer-as-hero and vendor-as-mentor. The "refusal" slide = the consulting objection-handling slide.

#### C4. Pixar Story Spine (Kenn Adams)
- **Structure**: Eight prompts: Once upon a time… / Every day… / Until one day… / Because of that… ×3 / Until finally… / And ever since then… (+ optional moral).
- **When to use**: Case studies, incident postmortems, retrospectives, customer-success stories — the fastest converter of chronology into causally-driven story. Also the best outline-QC tool for any narrative deck.
- **Slide mapping**: 8 beats ≈ 8–12 slides: world → stable routine → inciting break → 3+ causal-chain slides (one consequence each, ordered strictly by causality, not theme) → climax → new normal → moral/CTA.
- **Pitfalls**: Skipping "Every day" — two full beats on the ordinary world are what make the disruption land. "And then" lists instead of "because of that" chains. Diagnostic: if your slide titles won't slot into the spine prompts, you have a report, not a story.
- **Relatives**: The causality engine ("because of that") is the body-level version of ABT's single "but".

#### C5. Freytag's Pyramid
- **Structure**: Exposition → Rising Action → Climax (highest tension) → Falling Action → Denouement.
- **When to use**: Retrospectives, case studies, documentary-style "here is what happened" talks where the audience wants the full shape including aftermath. WEAK for live persuasion — a mid-deck climax means energy falls before the ask (exactly why Duarte redesigned it into the sparkline).
- **Slide mapping**: 1–2 context slides → escalating evidence (weakest→strongest) → ONE climax slide at ~60% (the single most-designed slide) → 1–2 falling-action slides (brutally short — this is where decks die) → resolution + what-changed + CTA.
- **Pitfalls**: The descending half is a liability in live talks; either compress falling action to near zero or move the climax to ~85%.
- **Relatives**: Direct ancestor of the sparkline; Vonnegut's Icarus shape (C10) is a Freytag deck that ends on the fall.

#### C6. Kishōtenketsu (four-act, non-conflict)
- **Structure**: Ki (introduce topic honestly, no problem statement) → Shō (develop richly, WITHOUT escalating conflict) → Ten (twist: an unexpected juxtaposition or reframe — the same data cut differently, the overlooked detail) → Ketsu (reconcile everything into a new whole). Drama from recontextualization, not confrontation.
- **When to use**: Insight reveals, research readouts, data storytelling where the payload is a reframe; audiences/cultures where manufactured crisis framing reads as manipulative. The antidote to the obligatory burning platform.
- **Slide mapping**: Four roughly equal sections (12-slide deck): 1–3 establish; 4–6 develop with texture; 7–8 the twist ("everything you just saw is also true of X"); 9–12 what the reframe means + recommendation.
- **Pitfalls**: The ten only lands if the shō is genuinely interesting on its own, not filler-before-the-reveal. Western business audiences itch during shō ("where is this going?") — keep it tight and signal craft.
- **Relatives**: Proof that you need CONTRAST and surprise, not conflict; pairs with Situation–Opportunity (B2) for change-fatigued rooms.

#### C7. Monroe's Motivated Sequence
- **Structure**: (1) Attention — striking opener; (2) Need — prove the status quo unacceptable, make it personal; (3) Satisfaction — the solution + objection handling; (4) Visualization — the future WITH it (positive), WITHOUT it (negative), or both side-by-side (contrast method, strongest); (5) Action — one specific, immediately doable commitment.
- **When to use**: Action-oriented persuasion where you need commitment in the room: sales closes, fundraising appeals, safety/policy/behavior change. Success metric = "did they do the thing".
- **Slide mapping**: 1–2 attention slides → 2–4 need slides (evidence + "this is you") → 2–4 satisfaction slides → 1–2 visualization slides (with/without side by side) → 1 action slide (a step doable TODAY, plus first-step logistics).
- **Pitfalls**: Skipping Visualization — the most-skipped and most powerful step. "Think about it" is not an action step; the ask must be immediate and low-friction.
- **Relatives**: Monroe = ABT with the Therefore split into three (solution, future, ask); Visualization is the direct ancestor of Duarte's New Bliss.

#### C8. Hook–Meat–Payoff
- **Structure**: Hook (curiosity + reason to listen: question, story, bold claim) → Meat (content in AT MOST three sections) → Payoff (the reward, circling back to the hook verbatim).
- **When to use**: Universal fallback; short talks and webinars; when nothing more specialized fits. The minimum viable narrative.
- **Slide mapping**: Slide 1 = the hook, NOT a title/agenda slide (agenda comes after the hook, if at all). Body = 3 labeled sections of 3–5 slides. Final slide answers the opening question / completes the opening story in its original words.
- **Pitfalls**: A payoff that doesn't reference the hook — loop closure is what makes a talk feel authored. Expert move: write the payoff FIRST; if you can't state it, you don't have a talk yet.
- **Relatives**: Cousin of Story Grid's Hook–Build–Payoff; the payoff-callback is bookending (H8); BLUF-compatible — the hook can BE the bottom line.

#### C9. In Medias Res (opening overlay)
- **Structure**: Open at/near the climax (outcome withheld) → rewind once ("three weeks earlier…") → build back → replay/resolve the opening moment with the audience now equipped to feel it.
- **When to use**: Risky pitches, incident postmortems, graveyard-slot keynotes — when you must seize attention in 30 seconds AND the material genuinely contains a dramatic peak. Skip if no real peak exists.
- **Slide mapping**: Slide 1: the peak, image + one line ("3:04 AM. The pipeline is down. We're losing $40K a minute.") — outcome withheld. Slide 2: rewind marker. Body: any framework. Penultimate: return to the opening scene, reveal the outcome. Close: resolution + CTA.
- **Pitfalls**: Rewinding more than once confuses live audiences. Burning the outcome on slide 1 kills the middle. Manufactured drama backfires.
- **Relatives**: An opening overlay on any body framework; the strongest form of the curiosity-gap opening (H6).

#### C10. Vonnegut's Story Shapes (diagnostic lens)
- **Structure**: Plot each slide's emotional altitude over time. Six empirically confirmed arcs (Reagan et al. 2016): Rags-to-Riches, Tragedy, Man-in-a-Hole (fall→rise), Icarus (rise→fall), Cinderella, Oedipus. Man-in-a-Hole — ending HIGHER than the start — is the default, highest-rated business arc.
- **When to use**: Not a build framework — a shape-check AFTER drafting any deck, and a chooser between fear-led (fall-first) and aspiration-led (rise-first) narratives.
- **Slide mapping**: Man-in-Hole deck: open at baseline → descend into the problem (the hole) → climb out via the solution → end ABOVE the starting line. Audit: a flat line = a report, not a talk.
- **Pitfalls**: The most common accidental shape in corporate decks is Icarus — strong opening, then risks/caveats/appendix landing the final emotional beat on a downslope. Fix: move risk slides BEFORE the resolution, never after. Audiences follow you into the hole only if they trust a climb is coming — signal it early.
- **Relatives**: Duarte's "flatline" diagnosis of lifeless meetings is the same chart; peak-end (H4) explains why the ending altitude matters.

#### C11. Golden Circle (Why → How → What)
- **Structure**: Communicate inside-out: WHY (belief/purpose) first, HOW (differentiating approach) second, WHAT (products, features, asks) last.
- **When to use**: Vision/mission talks, brand and product launches, recruiting, change leadership — audiences to INSPIRE. Avoid for time-poor decision briefings, where answer-first wins. Do not mix the two orderings in one deck.
- **Slide mapping**: Slides 1–3: the belief ("We believe…"), no product mention. Middle: how the belief becomes method. Final third: the what, each item framed as evidence of the why. The why returns verbatim on the closing slide.
- **Pitfalls**: Sinek's neuroscience justification is loose, but the ordering works as pre-suasive framing. Test: if slide 1 could open a competitor's deck unchanged, the why isn't specific enough.
- **Relatives**: Why-first is discovery-led ordering (B3) applied to identity rather than evidence; the verbatim-return is bookending (H8).
