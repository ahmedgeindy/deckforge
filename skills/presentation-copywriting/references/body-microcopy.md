## 2. TAKEAWAYS, SUPPORT & BODY COPY

### 2.1 The title IS the takeaway (default)

One claim-bearing element per slide; a separate takeaway line is banned when the title is assertive — two claim-carriers compete and drift apart in edits [MC §2.1]. Exception: templates that mandate topic titles (formal/academic/regulatory) → the claim relocates to ONE takeaway line adjacent to the evidence, styled second-loudest, obeying all title rules [MC §2.2]. Bottom-of-slide takeaways are read-mode only — presented slides are glanced top-left-first [MC §2.3]. The chart-internal title slot defers: if the slide title asserts, the chart title becomes the descriptive subtitle; never two stacked assertions [MC §2.4].

### 2.2 The precision split (title ↔ subtitle)

The title speaks plain language; ONE descriptive subtitle carries the technical statement — measure, units, population, timeframe (Economist/FT + Datawrapper convergence). Precision in the headline kills glanceability; precision nowhere kills trust. Every Evidence-archetype slide gets the subtitle; assertion titles never carry units or methodology [MC §1]. This is also where compressed scope qualifiers legally land (translation-procedure §6.3).

### 2.3 Body copy & bullets (wording layer; design §3 owns form)

- Bullets are a last resort upstream; when they survive: 1 line each, front-loaded, grammatically parallel FORM for genuine peers, **length varied with importance** (uniform length is the most reliable machine-text tell; target word-count CV ≥0.2) [MC §6; design Tells-C3]. Never pad an item to match its siblings — padding is where filler adjectives enter. One deliberate parallelism break per deck = an emphasis device; twice = noise. Anaphora belongs in the spoken track, not on the canvas (MED).
- **One benefit rung per slide** (registers §5.1): a slide stating feature AND advantage AND emotional benefit is three registers colliding — split or cut [MP §3.2].
- **The 30-second ceiling** on any value-prop block: speakable in one breath or it is over-explained — cut qualifiers first, then clauses [MP §4.3].
- Sentence length for read-mode prose: NNg prose rules govern slidedocs/notes; presented-slide copy is stricter (fragments + assertion titles) [TT conflict 6].

### 2.4 Claim–evidence pairing in copy [MP §7]

- **Adjacency contract**: every claim gets its proof in the same breath — same slide, same sentence where possible. A claim slide and a proof slide three slides apart = an unsupported claim plus an orphan exhibit.
- **One claim, one proof.** Stacking proofs signals doubt (over-proving is a hedge in disguise); best proof on the slide, second-best to appendix with a scent line.
- **Proof-genre ladder by register** (strongest first; proof persuades in proportion to similarity-to-audience and verifiability):
  - Presales: live demo / reproducible benchmark > named-customer metric with conditions > architecture walkthrough > third-party audit/cert > analyst quote (weakest — engineers discount analysts).
  - Exec: peer-company named outcome > quantified pilot on THEIR data > industry benchmark > vendor benchmark.
  - Marketing: named customer + number > recognizable logo + verbatim quote > aggregate stat > award/badge (near-zero).
  Choice of WHICH proof is this skill's; WHERE it sits is upstream (Cialdini via structure H3).
- **Quote craft**: quotes must contain a specific, ideally a number; edit for length, never for claims; attribute name + role + company — unattributed quotes are negative proof. Prefer factual paraphrase with attribution over an anonymous quote.
- **Guarantee/risk-reversal phrasing** sits adjacent to the CTA, restating the main promise while removing the risk; the B2B form = pilot terms, exit clauses, SLA numbers (Sugarman) [MP §7.4].

### 2.5 The preemptive claim & the demonstration-as-copy rule [MP §2.2–2.3]

- **Describing your process specifically beats claiming superiority generically** (Hopkins' preemptive claim): a concrete, truthful mechanism/practice statement — even one competitors could technically also make — persuades harder than a comparative adjective, because a specific process claim is self-evidencing: the reader supplies the conclusion themselves. "Platitudes and generalities roll off the human understanding like water from a duck" (Hopkins, *Scientific Advertising*; Schlitz case HIGH, figures MED).
- Slide application: on differentiation slides where honest feature parity exists — name YOUR verifiable practice rather than an unqualified quality adjective. Ethics guard: preemptive ≠ implying exclusivity; the presales register must survive the audience knowing competitors do it too — the claim is about being the one who shows the work.
- **Only claim what you can demonstrate; the demonstration IS the copy**: convert adjectives to their evidence — speed to the benchmark, ease to the step count, trust to the named customer + metric. If no evidence exists in the fact bank, the adjective is deleted, not softened (Hopkins; Ogilvy; converges with translation-procedure §6.5 and blacklist §8-B).

### 2.6 Exec-summary prose claims (the one non-title copy surface with its own rules)

Slide-2 exec summaries (structure B4) are two-tier prose: BOLD sentences carry claims (~125 chars each, BCG practice), indented light bullets carry data. Copy rules that apply here and nowhere else: each bold sentence is a full assertion obeying all §1 rules at the read-mode budget; reading ONLY the bold sentences must deliver the complete argument (skim test); each bold claim maps 1:1 to a body section in order and uses the message-house vocabulary verbatim — the exec summary is where competing paraphrases most often creep in, because it is written last [MP §0.2; structure B4].

### 2.7 The value-proposition wording contract [MP §4]

- **Fill Moore's positioning template BEFORE any headline is written** — "For [target] who [need], [product] is a [category] that [key benefit]. Unlike [primary alternative], it [primary differentiation]." It forces the four decisions copy otherwise re-litigates per slide (who, category, #1 benefit, vs-what); undetermined slots surface later as vague slides. The filled template lives in the message house, NEVER verbatim on a slide — template English is recognizable and inert (Moore, *Crossing the Chasm*).
- **Category-noun discipline**: one category noun, used identically on title, dividers, and close — the category noun is what the audience repeats to absentees (SKILL.md core idea 6).
- **Anti-superlative default**: "leading / best-in-class / next-generation" carry zero information; replace with the Moore differentiation-slot content (what you do that the alternative doesn't) or delete [MP §4.4; anti-patterns §8-1].
- Every slide headline should be derivable from one template slot; a headline that maps to no slot is either off-message (cut) or evidence the positioning is incomplete (escalate to the message house, not to wording).

### 2.8 Problem/value phrasing devices (JTBD) [MP §5]

- Problem beats: **job-story wording** — "When [concrete, recognizable situation], [they] need to [verb phrase they'd say aloud], so they can [their success metric]". Circumstance-framing is face-saving; "you have a problem" framing is defensive-making (converges with Raskin D7).
- Value beats: **outcome statements** (Ulwick form) — "[minimize/increase] the [metric] it takes to [job step]"; solution-agnostic, measurable, and they generate the before/after pairs Comparison slides need. (Klement–Ulwick theory feud noted; the devices are compatible — anti-patterns conflict 12.)
- **Vocabulary sourcing**: job wording uses the audience's own nouns/verbs — their system names, KPI names, navigation strings. A renamed workflow is the fastest outsider tell.

## 3. MICRO-COPY

### 3.1 Kickers [MC §5.7–5.9]

1–3 words, category not claim, no verb (a verb-bearing kicker is a second claim). Never repeats the title's words (echo = AI tell). If caps (design owns the one caps role): ≤3 words. The kicker's job is what lets the title be pure argument.

### 3.2 Captions [MC §3]

- Captions earn their place ONLY by adding what pixels can't say: identity, context, stakes, provenance. Never restate the visible or repeat the title.
- Two-sentence pattern: sentence 1 identifies (who/what/where/when, PRESENT tense); sentence 2 contextualizes into the slide's argument. Budget 1–2 sentences.
- **Presented decks mostly need NO captions** (title + narration already do both jobs); captions belong to read/sent-ahead decks and provenance-bearing evidence (screenshots, third-party exhibits). Screenshot caption formula: state + the one noteworthy element (MED, derived).
- Tone matches image gravity (no wit on incident exhibits). Third-party exhibits carry origin in micro role.

### 3.3 Annotations / callouts [MC §4]

- ≤10 words; ≤2 per chart (count/placement owned by design §5.3).
- **Must contain the operative fact — a number, delta, or cause — never a description of the shape.** A pointer without the fact is a treasure hunt.
- Earning types: outlier explanation, event marker, delta statement, artifact definition. Fragments legal, verbs preferred; falsifiability applies at annotation scale — if it could sit on any chart, cut it.
- Never a title paraphrase: the title carries the global claim, the annotation the local evidence. No exclamation points, no "note that…" (3 dead words of the 10). Same authorial voice as the title.

### 3.4 Axis & data labels [MC §5]

Units at the point of decoding ("3.4%", "$20k" — never a "figures in millions" multiplier note). Abbreviated numerals (20k/20m/20b); no trailing zeros; ≤3 sig figs (Tufte upstream). **Never rotate label text — rewrite it** (or flip to horizontal bars: a copy problem solved by chart choice). Unusual units spelled out on first use. Category labels in the audience's vocabulary. Every label front-loaded. Axis titles are often deletable once the subtitle states measure + units + scope.

### 3.4b Source lines [MC §7; design §5.3]

Micro role, fixed pattern deck-wide ("Source: name, year" class); every data exhibit and every third-party image carries one. Sourced exhibits signal rigor and are audit-proof; the source line is also the legal landing spot for compressed test conditions (translation-procedure §6.3) when the subtitle is full — reachable from the slide, never only in a document nobody opens. Weasel attributions never appear here (anti-patterns §8-5): a source line names a source or does not exist.

### 3.5 CTA wording [MP §8] (placement/structure upstream: C1/C7/F4; design §8.1)

- **Verb-first, outcome-flavored**: the verb names the value received, not the effort ("see/start/get [outcome]" class beats "contact/learn more" class — direction HIGH, magnitudes MED).
- **Next-24-hours specificity**: name what literally happens after yes; a concrete first step shrinks perceived commitment.
- **Friction-rank the verb** (see/get/try < start/schedule < buy/sign/commit); choose the lowest-commitment verb that still advances the deck's decision — skeptical rooms lowest, aligned exec rooms the real ask (under-asking a ready room wastes the meeting).
- **First-person possessive grammar only where a literal clickable/QR action exists** (webinar/marketing); spoken decks use audience-verb form (anti-patterns conflict 11).
- **Deadline and owner live IN the CTA sentence**; undated CTAs are wishes (structure rule 15).
- **One CTA phrasing, repeated VERBATIM at the post-Q&A re-close** — two paraphrases of the ask = two asks [MP §8.4].

### 3.6 Alt text (authored at copywriting time, not bolted on at QA) [MC §9]

- Every meaningful visual gets alt text; decorative elements are marked decorative. Silence reads as filenames.
- Alt = content + purpose, 1–2 sentences; never "image of…"; never repeats adjacent slide text (it is heard in sequence with the title).
- **Chart formula: form + subject + takeaway** — the encoding named, then the same conclusion a sighted glancer gets; it must agree with the title in substance while adding form and key values. Complex exhibits: short alt + long description in notes/appendix (WAI two-part pattern).
- Riding duties: unique title on every slide (screen-reader navigation is title-based — duplicated titles strand the listener); meaningful link text (never raw URLs or "click here"); reading order checked so the assertion title is encountered first; tables get real header rows, no merged cells [MC §9.6].
- Alt obeys the same voice and honesty rules as the visible layer — it asserts what the evidence shows, so the falsifiability test applies.

### 3.7 Casing & numerals [MC §10]

- **Sentence case deck-wide, every element.** Four independent mechanisms: assertion titles ARE sentences (Title Case Formats Labels); word-shape reading speed; proper-noun disambiguation (every capital becomes information); one-rule consistency (title case has competing rulebooks → guaranteed drift). Sanctioned exception: the single letter-spaced caps kicker role (design §7-18).
- Title case only under brand/template mandate — then ONE named convention deck-wide, and chart-internal text stays sentence case regardless.
- **Numerals as digits** in all slide copy including titles (glanceable, comparable); spell only at sentence start or in idioms (MED).

### 3.8 Micro-copy budgets ledger [MC §7]

| Element | Budget |
|---|---|
| Assertion title (presented) | ≤10 words, ≤2 lines, 1 claim, has verb |
| Action title (read mode) | ≤15 words, 2-line cap |
| Statement line | ≤6 words |
| Kicker | 1–3 words, no verb |
| Subtitle / chart description | 1 line: measure, units, scope |
| Annotation | ≤10 words, contains number/cause; ≤2 per chart |
| Caption | 1–2 sentences |
| Bullet item | 1 line, front-loaded; lengths varied |
| Source line | "Source: name, year" — micro role |
| Alt text (short) | 1–2 sentences |
| Notes one-liner | 1 spoken sentence, 8–16 words |
| Talk track | ~130–150 words per minute of slide dwell |

Budgets are ceilings, not targets; overflow → cut or split (`BUDGET_OVERFLOW`), never shrink.
