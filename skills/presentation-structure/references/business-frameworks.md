### B. Answer-first / Pyramid family (consulting & executive)

#### B1. Minto Pyramid Principle
- **Structure**: Governing thought (the single answer) at the apex → key line of ~3 MECE arguments (mutually exclusive, collectively exhaustive) → base of evidence. Vertical logic: each statement raises exactly one Why?/How? that the level below answers. Horizontal logic: ideas in a group are the same kind, summarizable in one sentence, ordered by time, structure, or importance. Think bottom-up, communicate top-down.
- **When to use**: Any recommendation-bearing deck for decision-makers (strategy, business cases, board updates). NOT for exploratory working sessions where no answer exists yet.
- **Slide mapping**: Governing thought = exec summary slide (slide 2). Key-line points = deck sections (each = section divider + line of the exec summary). Each key-line point → 3–7 body slides whose action titles answer the Why/How it raised. Typical 20–30 slide deck: title + exec summary + 3 sections × 5–8 slides + next steps + appendix.
- **Pitfalls**: Deductive chains at the top level — they make execs wait and collapse entirely if one link is challenged; use inductive (3 parallel independent arguments) at the key line, deduction only within sections. Blank headers ("Issues include..."). "Background" sections signal unsynthesized thinking. If the exec rejects the top line in minute one, everything after is tainted — mitigate by pre-wiring (B5).
- **Relatives**: BLUF is the military twin. Action titles (G1) are the pyramid made visible on slides. The exec summary (B4) is the pyramid's top two levels on one page.

#### B2. SCQA / SCR introduction (and its full-deck variants)
- **Structure**: Situation (facts the audience already agrees with — "remind, don't inform") → Complication (what changed; makes status quo untenable) → Question (the one question the complication forces, usually implied) → Answer (governing thought). Four orderings by audience: Standard S-C-A (builds the case), Direct A-S-C (answer first, impatient execs), Concerned C-S-A (open on the crisis), Aggressive Q-S-C-A (open on the question).
- **When to use**: The opening of every consulting deck, exec summary, memo. Choose ordering by audience stance.
- **Slide mapping**: Either compressed onto the exec summary slide (S and C = one ~125-character sentence each, real BCG practice), or expanded as slides 2–4 (one Situation slide, one Complication slide, Question as transition). Everything after the intro is Answer.
- **Pitfalls**: #1 failure: a Situation the audience disputes — the intro must be 100% agreeable so all argument happens in the Answer. #2: a Complication that doesn't force the exact question the deck answers. The Complication need not be negative ("we solved X — now what?" is valid).
- **Relatives**: SCQA is a compressed story — Situation = ordinary world, Complication = inciting incident, Answer = resolution moved to the front. The seed technique "Situation–Complication" = SCQA played at full deck length with the answer withheld (discovery-led). "Situation–Opportunity" = SCQA with an aspirational, gain-framed complication — use for change-fatigued audiences where a burning platform would poison the room. SCR is McKinsey's working shorthand for the same thing.

#### B3. BLUF / answer-first vs discovery-led ordering
- **Structure**: BLUF (US Army AR 25-50): bottom line — conclusion, recommendation, ask — in the first sentence/slide, support after. Discovery-led is the inverse: evidence first, conclusion at the end.
- **When to use**: Answer-first is the DEFAULT for executives (limited attention; they interrupt and skip ahead anyway; it lets them spend meeting time on the parts they doubt). Discovery-led only deliberately: hostile factions, recommendations that indict the audience's own past decisions, or when demonstrating objectivity matters.
- **Slide mapping**: Answer-first: slide 2 = recommendation, body = support, closer = decision ask. Discovery-led: slides 2–3 = agreed situation and method, body = evidence chain, recommendation on the penultimate slide. Same evidence slides — only placement of the answer moves.
- **Pitfalls**: Answer-first on a hostile room makes resistance HARDER (documented in persuasion research — early explicit claims increase defensiveness). Discovery-led on an impatient exec loses them before the answer arrives.
- **Relatives**: Direct-vs-indirect approach in business-communication literature; the hostile-audience playbook (F4) is discovery-led taken to its limit.

#### B4. The Executive Summary slide (SCR bold-claim construction)
- **Structure**: One slide, ≤~400 words: Situation 10–15%, Complication 15–20%, Resolution 60–70% (recommendation + its ~3 key-line supports). Two-tier hierarchy: BOLD sentences carry claims, indented light bullets carry data. RSC variant (Resolution first) when the audience is already aligned.
- **When to use**: Slide 2 of virtually every consulting/board deck. Write it assuming it's the ONLY slide that gets read.
- **Slide mapping**: Skim test: reading only the bold sentences delivers the complete argument. Each bold claim maps 1:1 to a body section, in order — the exec summary is the deck's table of contents expressed as an argument.
- **Pitfalls**: Table-of-contents summaries (topics not conclusions); buried recommendations; hedging verbs ("consider exploring" — say "invest $2M in X by Q3"); claims without numbers; drafting it before the analysis is done. If it spills past one slide, prioritization isn't finished. Written LAST, placed FIRST.
- **Relatives**: Duarte confirms the same shape from the story side: her exec-summary advice is a three-act structure (current reality → problem/opportunity → recommendation) — SCR is a compressed story.

#### B5. Appendix system + pre-wiring
- **Structure**: Main deck stays lean (only slides that pass the so-what test and sit on the storyline). Appendix = 5–10 slides, each pre-answering one NAMED anticipated question, across five types: methodology, deeper data cuts, alternative scenarios, historical benchmarks, risks/mitigations. Pre-wiring: socialize key ideas 1:1 with each decision-maker BEFORE the meeting.
- **When to use**: Every executive/board deck. The deeper the technical scrutiny, the larger the appendix.
- **Slide mapping**: Appendix sits after the ask slide behind a divider; slides numbered (A1, A2…) and cross-referenced from body slides; each retrievable in <15 seconds live; may be denser than body slides (read silently while you talk).
- **Pitfalls**: Treating the appendix as a dump for cut content — each slide is built FORWARD from a predicted question (red-team session generates them). Surprises in a board room kill trust — pre-wire contentious topics, always.
- **Relatives**: Duarte's exec guidance (ask the exec beforehand about appendix/density/pre-read preferences); Hoffman's steer-into-objections for pitches (D3).

### E. Teaching & explanation

#### E1. The Explanation
- **Structure**: (1) Context: where we are, where we need to get to → (2) Relevance: why this affects THIS audience — their reason to listen → (3) step-by-step walkthrough with comprehension checkpoints → (4) Arrival: the new understanding/capability, stated.
- **When to use**: Informative decks: new processes, regulations, tools, onboarding, all-hands explainers — understanding, not persuasion.
- **Slide mapping**: Slide 1: from-here-to-there roadmap diagram. Slide 2: "what this means for you" (per role if audiences differ). Body: one step per slide, numbered, rigidly consistent layout, progress indicators ("step 3 of 7"), checkpoint recap every 3–4 steps. Close: journey restated as completed + where to get help.
- **Pitfalls**: Relevance-before-steps is load-bearing: without a personal "so what," audiences can't hold procedural steps in working memory. Checkpoints double as re-entry points for drifted listeners.
- **Relatives**: The neutral-audience arc where the conclusion emerges midway; Gagné (E2) is its rigorous instructional-design big sibling.

#### E2. Gagné's Nine Events + Mayer's segmenting (training loop)
- **Structure**: Per learning objective: gain attention → state the objective → recall prior knowledge → present material → guidance/worked examples → elicit PRACTICE → feedback → assess → transfer. Mayer: learner-paced segments, strip extraneous material, words+pictures beat words alone.
- **When to use**: Training decks, workshops, certifications — anything whose success metric is what the audience can DO afterward.
- **Slide mapping**: Per objective (one module): attention/scenario slide → "by the end you will be able to…" → recall slide → 3–6 content slides → worked example → practice slide (the deck literally STOPS for activity) → feedback/answers → mini-assessment. 3–5 objectives max per session. Closer: transfer slide (where they'll apply it next week).
- **Pitfalls**: Training decks built like keynotes — density without practice loops. A deck with no stopping points isn't training. Put segment boundaries where interaction tools (polls, exercises) fire.
- **Relatives**: Mayer's pre-training principle (G3) = Gagné's recall step; checkpoints in E1 are lightweight Gagné assessment events.
