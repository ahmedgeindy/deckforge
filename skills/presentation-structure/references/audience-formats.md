# Audience & Format Playbooks (F)

> Reference file of the `presentation-structure` skill. Framework codes (A1, B2, C1...) are stable across SKILL.md and all reference files.

Note: webinar/all-hands timings (F8, F9) are practitioner consensus, not research-backed — treat as defaults, not laws.

### F. Audience & format playbooks

#### F1. Executives (Duarte's 5 rules + the 10% rule)
- **Structure**: Summarize up front (conclusion + recommendation + ask first). Set expectations for talk vs discussion. If the slot is 30 minutes, build the presented portion as if it's 5. **10% rule**: summary slides ≈ 10% of total deck; everything else is appendix. Rehearse being interrupted and jumping to any appendix slide on demand.
- **Slide mapping**: Slides 1–5 = the entire argument (answer, 3 supports, ask). Slides 6–50+ = appendix, never presented linearly. NO agenda slide — the summary IS the agenda. Slide 1 answers the exact question you were invited to answer.
- **Pitfalls**: Treating the slot as airtime to fill. Saying "I'll get to that in a later slide" — jump to it. For execs, discussion IS the main event; slides are reference material; continuous Q&A is the format, so design the deck to be interrupted.

#### F2. Board meetings (Sequoia/Schreier + pre-wiring)
- **Structure**: Send a dense pre-read 2–7 days ahead, built from the reports you already run the company on. Meeting: Big Picture/CEO update 15 min (highlights, lowlights, asks) → Calibration 45–60 min (fewest correct metrics to re-immerse: financials+forecast, sales vs targets, engagement/retention/NPS) → Company Building 30 min (forward org chart, roadmap, waterfalls) → Working Sessions 30 min/topic (where you actually want input) → closed session.
- **Slide mapping**: The deck is a pre-read slidedoc, not a stage deck; in-meeting you do NOT walk it linearly — spend the time on working-session topics. Amazon-style memos are an accepted substitute.
- **Pitfalls**: Two iron rules: (a) the board should discuss, not be presented at; (b) NO surprises — pre-wire contentious topics in 15–30 min 1:1 calls with each director; the meeting confirms and pressure-tests. Boards' biggest deficit is context — front-load calibration before asking for judgment.

#### F3. Technical/peer audiences (broad–narrow–broad hourglass)
- **Structure**: Intro (broad): the problem the field cares about + your one-sentence claim UP FRONT (BLUF still applies to peers). Body (narrow): background → method → results → limitations, defined terms, honest constraints. Conclusion (broad): what it means for the field, what's next.
- **Slide mapping**: 1–2 problem + claim; 3 roadmap (acceptable here, unlike exec decks); 4–6 background/definitions; 7–12 method and evidence, one result per slide, an explicit limitations slide; 13 implications. Back-pocket appendix of 5–15 anticipated-question slides (ablations, edge cases, raw data).
- **Pitfalls**: Technical audiences distrust polish without limitations — the limitations slide INCREASES credibility. Never bury the result for suspense; peers want the claim early to evaluate evidence against it. For mixed rooms, layer: big picture → strategic detail → deep dive/appendix.

#### F4. Hostile/skeptical audiences (delayed thesis, Rogerian)
- **Structure**: (1) Common ground — shared goals, where you agree with THEM → (2) their position stated fairly (steelman), conceding genuinely valid points → (3) evidence walked step by step BEFORE any ask → (4) your claim introduced late, once evidence makes it feel inevitable → (5) remaining objections rebutted two-sidedly → (6) the SMALLEST acceptable next step.
- **Slide mapping**: Slide 1 shared goal ("we all want X"); 2 their view, often verbatim; 3 "where you're right"; 4–8 evidence chain, one link per slide; 9 the claim (first appearance); 10–11 objection→rebuttal pairs; closer = modest ask, door open. This EXACTLY INVERTS answer-first.
- **Pitfalls**: The one audience where BLUF backfires — early explicit claims make resistant listeners MORE defensive. Two-sided messages beat one-sided for educated or opposed audiences. Win condition is "willing to hear more," never full conversion in one talk.

#### F5. Long keynotes (Medina 10-minute blocks + sparkline overlay)
- **Structure**: Attention decays at ~10-minute intervals. Chunk a 45–60 min talk into 4–5 modules: 1 minute stating one core concept, ~9 minutes of detail traceable to it, then an emotionally competent hook at the boundary (story, demo, video, question) that buys the next 10 minutes and ideally foreshadows the next block.
- **Slide mapping**: Module-opener slide = the one-sentence concept; 6–10 detail slides; hook slide between modules; ≤10 slides between emotional resets. Overlay the sparkline: each module can be one what-is/what-could-be oscillation; end on CTA then new-bliss image.
- **Pitfalls**: Irrelevant jokes as hooks backfire — relevance separates a hook from a gimmick. Never run two data-dense modules back to back. Virtual audiences decay ~2x faster — halve the interval (F10).

#### F6. TED-style 18-minute talks
- **Structure**: One throughline (≤15 words), 1–3 concepts max, each shown to matter then fleshed out with concrete stories. Common arc: personal connection → the idea → evidence/stories → implication for the audience.
- **Slide mapping**: Slides optional and sparse (image-dominant, zero bullets, ~10–20 slides or none). Opener: inside a story or surprising fact within 60 seconds — no agenda, no self-intro beyond a line. Closer: return to the opening with the idea installed.
- **Pitfalls**: 18 minutes forces choosing ONE idea — that's why it works. Cut topics, not depth.

#### F7. Constrained short formats (lightning / Ignite / PechaKucha)
- **Structure**: Lightning (5 min, manual): hook → 3–4 points → takeaway, 3–7 slides. Ignite: 20 slides × 15s auto. PechaKucha: 20 × 20s = 6:40; arc: slides 1–3 context, 4–6 tension, 7–14 development, 15–17 resolution, 18–20 close. Script ~45–55 words per 20s slide.
- **Pitfalls**: The classic failure is compressing a 30-min talk instead of choosing a 5-min idea — overview is the win condition. Auto-advance decks are metronomes: rehearse against the timer; add image-only "buffer beats" after complex slides to absorb drift.

#### F8. Webinar conversion structure
- **Structure**: 60 min: Hook 0–7 (credibility + promised transformation + FIRST poll before minute 7) → Teach 7–35 (3–4 insights; poll ~15, chat prompt ~28) → Pivot 35–42 (customer transformation story; commitment prompt) → Offer 42–50 → Q&A 50–60 with the CTA on screen throughout. Interaction every 3–4 minutes.
- **Slide mapping**: 40–60 light slides for 45 min of content (visual change every 20–30s). Slides 1–2 = the transformation promise, not the agenda. Dedicated poll slides at planned timestamps.
- **Pitfalls**: Attendees who interact in the first 10 minutes are ~3x likelier to stay for the offer; a first poll after minute 20 is too late. No housekeeping in the first 5 minutes — that's the highest-attention window.

#### F9. All-hands / town halls
- **Structure**: 45 min max, 3–5 timed topics: opening story/context (what changed) → performance vs goals (honest lowlights included) → team spotlights and recognition → what's next + specific asks → anonymous-enabled Q&A → one-slide restatement of the theme AFTER Q&A.
- **Slide mapping**: Slide 1 = a one-line theme (not "Q3 All Hands"); one metric per slide with commentary; faces on recognition slides; final slide re-closes on your message.
- **Pitfalls**: Recognition is structural, not decorative — it's the segment employees remember. Q&A quality gates trust: collect questions anonymously beforehand, answer the hard ones first. Rotate presenters for energy.

#### F10. Remote/hybrid delivery
- **Structure**: Attention decays ~2x faster online: 5–8 minute content chunks, something changing on screen every 20–30 seconds (build, slide, annotation), interaction every 5–10 minutes, first interactive moment before minute 7, continuous content capped at 20–25 min.
- **Slide mapping**: MORE slides, less per slide: a 30-min in-person deck of 15 slides becomes 40–60 lighter slides/builds online. Hybrid: design for the remote attendee; repeat room questions aloud; every key point on a slide.
- **Pitfalls**: Static slides online = an invitation to check email. The 20–30 second visual-change rule is the single highest-leverage adaptation.

#### F11. Cold vs warm openings
- **Structure**: Cold room (no trust): connect before content → relevance to THEM in 60 seconds → one concrete credibility line (not a bio slide) → then the hook/idea; avoid pitch language early. Warm room: skip credentialing, reference shared history, open with the delta or the answer.
- **Slide mapping**: Cold: slide 1 = about THEM or the shared problem (never "About Me"); slide 2 = one-line credibility; slide 3 = the hook/claim. Warm: slide 1 = the delta since last time or the answer itself; zero credibility slides.
- **Pitfalls**: Cold-room credibility is best delivered by a third party (script the host's introduction). Warm-room failure = re-explaining known context — it reads as padding and burns the highest-attention minutes.

#### F12. Q&A placement (the 3/4 rule)
- **Structure**: Default: Q&A at ~75% of the way through, then a planned strong close AFTER the last question — the final words in the room are always yours. Variants: execs = continuous Q&A; large keynotes = moderated at end with a planted first question; webinars = dedicated block before the CTA; hostile rooms = written/moderated questions to prevent hijack.
- **Slide mapping**: Q&A slide before the final 1–2 closing slides, never last. Keep a re-close slide queued after Q&A; prepare a self-ask ("a question I'm often asked…") for silent rooms. Final on-screen slide as people leave = your message or CTA, never "Thank You / Questions?".
- **Pitfalls**: Ending on a random audience question surrenders the recency slot. Bridge: answer the final question, then pivot — "which brings me back to the one thing to remember…".

#### F13. Slidedoc vs stage deck vs memo (the artifact decision)
- **Structure**: Stage deck = spoken artifact: sparse, image-led, one idea/slide, meaningless without the speaker. Slidedoc (Duarte) = read artifact: full-sentence headline + ~75–100 words per page, self-sufficient; consulting-style pages ARE slidedocs. Amazon 6-pager = prose narrative (no bullets) + PR/FAQ, meeting opens with silent reading — the anti-slide option for the highest-stakes decisions.
- **When to use**: Classify BEFORE authoring. Pre-reads, board packs, leave-behinds → slidedoc or memo. Live talks → stage deck. Need both → produce TWO artifacts (each sparse stage slide typically expands into one slidedoc page by writing the talk track into it).
- **Pitfalls**: The #1 corporate deck failure is the hybrid "franken-deck"/slideument — too dense to present, too thin to read. Bezos's charge against bullets: they "flatten relative importance and ignore interconnectedness" — full-sentence claims keep logic inspectable. If evidence density is what matters, send a memo and keep slides for shared focus (Tufte, G5).
- **Relatives**: Presented vs read is the same axis as Alley's presented-vs-read distinction (G1) and Kawasaki's send-ahead caveat (D2).
