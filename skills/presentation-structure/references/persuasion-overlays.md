### H. Persuasion & memory overlays (apply to any framework)

#### H1. Ethos–Logos–Pathos placement (classical arrangement)
- **Structure**: Ethos EARLY (one credibility marker creates the hook logos hangs on); logos as the SPINE of the middle; pathos peppered throughout and CONCENTRATED at the close (the classical peroration was explicitly the emotional appeal).
- **Slide mapping**: Slides 1–3: hook + ONE credibility line (full credentials → appendix). Middle: each evidence slide pairs a claim with a human consequence. One refutatio slide (strongest objection, answered) after the evidence, before the close. Penultimate: emotional peroration. Final: the ask.
- **Pitfalls**: A long "about us" opening reads as insecurity and wastes the primacy slot. Voluntarily raising the strongest objection builds more ethos than hiding it.

#### H2. SUCCESs / Made to Stick (property checklist, not a sequence)
- **Structure**: Simple (find the core, lead with it) · Unexpected (break the guessing machine to GET attention; open a knowledge gap to KEEP it) · Concrete (photographable specifics — dual coding gives two retrieval paths; the antidote to the expert's Curse of Knowledge) · Credible (Sinatra-test flagship example; testable claims; statistics rescaled to human terms) · Emotional (one identifiable person, not masses) · Stories (simulation + inspiration).
- **When to use**: Final QA checklist on any deck; generative guide for idea-selling and change decks.
- **Slide mapping**: Core sentence on title AND final slide; slide 2 violates an expectation and poses the question the deck answers; every abstract claim paired with a tangible instance; one "if it worked at X, it works anywhere" case slide; stakes shown via one named person; challenge-plot story opened early, resolved at the close.
- **Pitfalls**: Concreteness test per headline: "could you photograph this?" — rewrite "significant operational inefficiencies" as "three warehouses re-key the same order four times". Vivid incidental detail also functions as a truth cue (serves logos and ethos simultaneously).

#### H3. Cialdini principles as slide-placement choices
- **Structure**: Persuasion is sequencing — each principle answers a different silent audience question at a different moment. Pre-suasion: the slide immediately BEFORE the ask shapes the decision more than the ask slide itself.
- **Slide mapping**: Title/opening image primes the frame the ask needs. Authority: one early marker (slides 2–3). Reciprocity: a genuinely useful insight in the first third before asking anything. Commitment/consistency: an early problem-framing slide that earns an explicit "yes, that's our situation," cashed in by a recap before the close. Social proof: IMMEDIATELY AFTER the solution reveal (when they're asking "does this work?") — never before the problem; peer-similar proof beats prestigious-but-distant logos. Scarcity: penultimate zone, framed as LOSS ("cost of waiting"). Unity: "we" language at open and close.
- **Pitfalls**: Don't stack all seven — pick 2–3 matching the audience's live objection. Social proof placed too early answers a question nobody asked and gets discounted. Never let the slide before the ask be housekeeping.

#### H4. Peak-End Rule + the STAR moment
- **Structure**: Memory of an experience ≈ average of its PEAK and its ENDING; duration is largely neglected. Engineer exactly ONE peak (Duarte's S.T.A.R. — Something They'll Always Remember: prop, live demo, dramatized statistic, repeatable sound bite, emotive story) and engineer the ending (emotional close + crisp ask).
- **Slide mapping**: Storyboard the peak FIRST, before any other slide; place it ~60–80% through, dramatizing the core idea (Gates releasing mosquitoes at TED 2009; Jobs pulling the MacBook Air from an envelope). Final 2–3 slides: rising emotional close, then the ask as the last thing said.
- **Pitfalls**: The peak must MAGNIFY the message, not compete with it — an unrelated stunt is remembered instead of the idea. Two engineered peaks flatten into none. STAR moments are planned and rehearsed, never improvised. Duration neglect is the license to cut: removing 10 middle slides costs nothing in remembered value.

#### H5. Primacy–Recency architecture (serial position)
- **Structure**: First and last items are recalled far better than the middle; the middle sags. Cueing the ending ("here's what this all means") re-triggers attention.
- **Slide mapping**: Slides 1–2 = hook + core message — NEVER logistics, agenda-only, or about-us. Middle = methodology, caveats, detail (or appendix). Final slide = the core message or ask — never "Thank You / Questions?". Keep a re-close slide for after Q&A (Q&A usually occupies the true recency slot — the post-Q&A re-close is the highest-leverage slide most decks omit).
- **Pitfalls**: Serial position applies within bullet lists too: first and last bullets get read, middle bullets die — a 5-bullet slide effectively communicates two. Cap lists at three.

#### H6. Curiosity gap + open loops (Loewenstein / Zeigarnik)
- **Structure**: Curiosity fires when the audience becomes AWARE of a gap between what they know and want to know. A hook = setup (reveal a gap exists) + implied promise (the talk will close it). Open loops: unresolved questions stay cognitively active — open a master loop at the start, mini-loops at section boundaries, close small loops within 1–2 sections, close the master loop just before the ask; keep ≤3–5 loops live at once.
- **Slide mapping**: Slides 1–2 pose the gap (question, anomaly, half-told story, explicit promise). Reframe the agenda as three QUESTIONS, not topics. A specific later slide closes the exact gap slide 2 opened, in so many words.
- **Pitfalls**: A gap needs baseline knowledge — first make them realize what they don't know. Every loop must pay off: unclosed loops read as broken promises (the clickbait failure). Track loops in the outline as a two-column ledger (opened-at / closed-at); ship no orphans. Label the loop when you open it — unregistered loops generate no tension.

#### H7. Story before statistics (identifiable-victim effect)
- **Structure**: One identifiable person (name, face, detail) → emotional engagement → THEN scale up to the statistic. Evidence: donations to the named girl Rokia ~2x the statistical appeal; critically, ADDING statistics to her story LOWERED donations — analytic framing suppresses empathy. Heath's classroom study: 63% remember stories, 5% remember any statistic.
- **Slide mapping**: The problem section LEADS with a single named, pictured case; the aggregate goes on the NEXT slide ("…and there are 3 million like her"). In the close, return to the same person in the after-state.
- **Pitfalls**: The rule is SEQUENCE, DON'T BLEND — never merge story and statistic on one slide or in one appeal. One identified case beats two or more; specificity carries the effect. Rescale every statistic to human/sensory terms; at most one statistic per slide — the one you want remembered.

#### H8. Callback architecture: bookends + refrains
- **Structure**: Bookend: open with an element, leave it deliberately incomplete, return to it transformed at the close (story in two halves; question → answer; same image modified; same quote reinterpreted). Refrain (anaphora): a fixed verbal stem repeated ≥3 times with varied completions, escalating to the biggest final instance (MLK's "I have a dream" ×8) — after the second repetition the audience anticipates the pattern, and the anticipation IS the engagement.
- **Slide mapping**: Slide 1 and the final slide designed as a pair. A refrain slide — fixed phrase, fixed typography — returns between sections, completed differently each time; final recurrence largest, on or near the close.
- **Pitfalls**: The payoff must reference the hook in its ORIGINAL words — loop closure is what makes a talk feel authored rather than assembled. The split-story bookend is the strongest variant (Zeigarnik doing structural work). Repetition with VARIATION beats verbatim repetition.

#### H9. Rule of Three
- **Structure**: Three levels: macro — exactly 3 main sections; meso — 3 reasons/options/examples per argument; micro — tricolon phrasing ("of the people, by the people, for the people").
- **Slide mapping**: Roadmap: 3 items max. Body: 3 signposted sections. Summary restates the same 3 in the same words. Tagline written as a tricolon, recurring on title/refrains/close.
- **Pitfalls**: If content genuinely has 5+ points, group under 3 headers — the audience remembers the headers and retrieves the rest. In a tricolon the third slot carries the punch. Reconciliation with the 4-chunk limit: 3 is the default target, 4 the hard ceiling (see §2 conflicts).
