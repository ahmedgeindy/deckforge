### D. Pitch & sales skeletons

#### D1. The default investor skeleton (merged Sequoia / Kawasaki / YC)
- **Structure**: 10–14 slides: Title + one-sentence company purpose → Problem → Solution → Why Now → Market (bottom-up) → Product (one visual) → Traction → Business Model → Competition → Team → Financials (only if defensible) → Ask / Vision closer. Distinctives: Sequoia contributes the one-declarative-sentence opener and the Why Now slide (a concrete technological/regulatory/behavioral/economic inflection); YC/Ralston adds an explicit Vision slide up front and an explicit Fundraising-ask closer + a Summary slide of 3–5 takeaways that survives partial reads.
- **When to use**: Seed through Series A raises; the skeleton every stage-specific reorder plugs into.
- **Slide mapping**: Slide 1 = plain-language what-we-do (no mystery openers in investor decks). Slides 2–4 = the argument trio (problem → solution → why now). 5–7 = business case. 8–9 = team + numbers. Closer = ask (amount, what it buys, milestones to next round), optionally bookended by the 5-year vision echoing slide 1.
- **Pitfalls**: No Why Now = "why wasn't this built 5 years ago, and why won't it be obsolete in 5?". Top-down TAM math (investors distrust it — size bottom-up like Uber: rides/city × spend/ride; or validate with EXISTING behavior like Airbnb: 630K couchsurfers + 17K Craigslist listings proved demand and supply separately). "No competitors" claims. Product-before-pain ordering. Mirror problem and solution structurally (Airbnb's 3 problem bullets map 1:1 to 3 solution benefits).
- **Relatives**: Kawasaki 10/20/30 (D2) is this skeleton plus delivery constraints; "The Pitch" (Wind-up → Hurdle → Vision → Options → Close, from the seed article) is the same skeleton with a story-dramatized problem and an options-analysis section.

#### D2. Kawasaki 10/20/30
- **Structure**: 10 slides, 20 minutes, ≥30pt font. Order: Title; Problem/Opportunity; Value Prop; Underlying Magic (demo/diagram, not words); Business Model; Go-to-Market; Competition; Team; Financials & Metrics; Status/Timeline/Use of Funds.
- **When to use**: Live pitches with a ~60-minute slot (pitch 20, discuss 40); also his default for sales/partnership pitches. NOT for send-ahead decks.
- **Slide mapping**: Slide 4 (the magic/demo) is the credibility peak, placed early-middle. Slide 10 closer = traction + milestones + what the money buys.
- **Pitfalls**: The constraints ARE the point — a normal human absorbs ~10 concepts per meeting "and VCs are very normal". The 30pt floor is an anti-teleprompter device: text small enough to read aloud makes the audience read ahead and desync. Send-ahead versions need added market/roadmap/financial-narrative pages (they're read without you).
- **Relatives**: The 30pt floor doubles as the content governor behind the glance test (G2).

#### D3. Hoffman investment-thesis-led deck (LinkedIn Series B)
- **Structure**: Open with the 3–8 things "investors must believe to want to be shareholders," then prove each belief. Declare concept pitch (vision-led) vs data pitch (metrics-led) and structure accordingly. Steer into the 1–3 biggest objections EARLY. Pitch by analogy ("eBay for professional reputation") but don't reason by analogy. Include explicit risks + mitigations. Frame financing across rounds: this round's milestones set up the next round's story. 20–25 slides is his stated ceiling.
- **When to use**: Series A/B+, contrarian or pre-revenue businesses, sophisticated repeat investors who must be argued into a worldview.
- **Slide mapping**: Slides 1–2 = the thesis as numbered beliefs. Early slides = pre-empt killer objections. Middle = one section per belief. Include a risks-and-mitigations slide. Closer = ask framed as milestones that make the next round obvious.
- **Pitfalls**: Opening with the team slide even though team matters — thesis first, then who executes it. Hiding risks (naming them builds credibility; investors already thought of them).
- **Relatives**: A pitch is an argument about beliefs, not a tour of the company — the belief chain is Minto's key line (B1) applied to fundraising.

#### D4. Lead-from-strength ordering (the opener debate, resolved)
- **Structure**: Keep the D1 body; choose the OPENER by strongest signal and stage. Pre-seed: vision + founder-market fit (concept pitch — no data exists). Seed: classic problem → solution → early traction. Series A+: traction chart first (ARR/NRR/logos), problem becomes context. Category-creation/contrarian: world-change or investment-thesis first. Always state what the company literally does in the first 30 seconds regardless.
- **When to use**: Whenever ordering slides for a specific company — the meta-framework the templates plug into.
- **Slide mapping**: Slide 1 = title + plain what-we-do. Slide 2 = your single strongest card (pick ONE: traction graph, thesis, named shift, dream team, or classic problem). Slides 3–8 = remaining inventory in whatever order keeps the causal chain intact. Slide 9 = team (unless it led). Closer = ask + use of funds + milestones.
- **Pitfalls**: Your unfair advantage cannot sit at slide 8 — investors decide before slide 7; cold readers average 2:31 on the whole deck. Exemplars: Buffer moved traction to slides 3–4 as the deliberate hero ("focusing everything on the traction slide was probably our only way to raise"); Uber's 25-slide deck is remembered partly for skippable filler — cut when in doubt.
- **Relatives**: Problem-first stays safe for FUNDRAISING (the investor isn't the one with the problem) while being weak for SELLING (Raskin's defensiveness argument, D7) — the two contexts genuinely differ.

#### D5. DocSend investor-attention evidence (budget layer, not a template)
- **Structure**: Verified findings: average first read 3:44 (trending shorter); cold-sent 2:31 (~3–5% convert) vs warm-intro 4:18 (~40–50% convert); avg deck ~19 pages; ~30% of converting decks are forwarded internally before any meeting. Dwell-time ranking (2024): Team (~1:02) > Financials (52s) > Traction (49s) > Problem (38s) > Solution (33s) > Model (30s) > Market (27s) > Competition (22s) > Product (21s).
- **When to use**: Allocating craft effort per slide on any send-ahead deck; designing for the unattended sub-4-minute skim.
- **Slide mapping**: Spend disproportionate craft on Team, Financials/Traction, Competition. Product = one visual slide (least-read — investors buy team+traction+market, not features). Every slide parseable in <10 seconds; assertion headline on each so the internal-forward audience gets the argument with zero narration.
- **Pitfalls**: Counterintuitive: the TEAM slide gets the MOST attention — it must be genuinely strong (quantified credentials, founder-market fit), never filler. Financials are optional at seed, but if included become the most-scrutinized slide — only defensible numbers (Uber's worst/realistic/best scenario slide showed the founders had priced their own downside).
- **Relatives**: Resolves the team-slide placement conflict with Kawasaki/Hoffman — see §2 conflicts table.

#### D6. YC Demo Day rules (Kevin Hale) — short-format pitch
- **Structure**: Legible, simple, obvious. 5–7 slides for a 2:30 pitch (matching what an investor can remember). Order: what you do in plain words → problem with ONE number → solution as one simple visual → strongest traction metric (~30% of airtime) → one-line model → one-number market → team.
- **When to use**: Demo days and any 2–5 minute slot; the design laws generalize to every deck.
- **Slide mapping**: Huge bold type, high contrast, text at top (back of a 500-person room). Banned: screenshots (use a simplified product drawing or 15–20s clip), animations, transitions, memes, dense diagrams. Graph captions state the conclusion.
- **Pitfalls**: The "stranger test": someone with zero context must grasp each slide immediately. "Don't try to make the slides the thing they'll remember" — the memorable unit is the one-sentence claim per slide.
- **Relatives**: The demo-day laws are assertion-evidence (G1) + glance test (G2) under extreme time pressure.

#### D7. Raskin 5-element strategic sales narrative (Zuora deck)
- **Structure**: (1) Name the big relevant CHANGE in the world (never your product or "the problem") → (2) show there will be winners and losers (loss aversion, stakes) → (3) tease the Promised Land — the customer's desirable future state, as criteria, NOT your product → (4) introduce capabilities as "magic gifts" that carry the prospect past specific obstacles → (5) evidence you can make the story come true (customer success stories first, demo second).
- **When to use**: Sales decks, first calls, category creation — any pitch where the buyer's real competitor is the status quo. Widely adapted for late-stage fundraising narratives.
- **Slide mapping**: Slides 1–2 = the shift, named memorably ("the subscription economy"). 3–4 = winners vs losers with real names/data. 5 = Promised Land criteria on one slide. Middle = one gift per obstacle (demo lives HERE, mapped to what the features are FOR). Closer = proof story of someone like them who arrived + next step.
- **Pitfalls**: The core inversion: "when you assert prospects have a problem, you put them on the defensive" — the external change gives them a face-saving reason to act. The Promised Land must be desirable AND hard to reach without you. With savvy/expert prospects the "shift" must be genuinely non-obvious or it reads as theater.
- **Relatives**: Structurally the hero's journey (C3) with customer-as-hero, vendor-as-Yoda; the shift/winners-losers pair supplies the stakes that problem-first decks lack; same move as Duarte's what-is/could-be at sales scale.

#### D8. Dunford two-act sales pitch (Setup → Follow-Through)
- **Structure**: ACT 1: Market Insight (your unique POV that reframes how buyers should decide) → Alternatives (honest map with genuine pros AND cons; doubles as discovery) → the Perfect World (criteria an ideal solution would meet). ACT 2: product as embodiment of those criteria → demo mapped to DIFFERENTIATED value themes (value only you deliver) → proof → objections → the ask.
- **When to use**: B2B sales in crowded markets; confused buyers; when losses go mostly to "no decision" rather than to competitors.
- **Slide mapping**: Slides 1–2 insight; 3–4 alternatives landscape (fair pros/cons); 5 "so a perfect solution would…" criteria; 6 product reveal against the criteria; middle = demo sections each headlined by a differentiated-value theme; then proof + pre-empts; closer = concrete next step.
- **Pitfalls**: The most fearsome competitor is the status quo — the pitch's first job is making the buyer confident in HOW to decide. A demo not mapped to differentiated value is a feature tour. Dishonest alternatives analysis kills the credibility that makes your cons believed.
- **Relatives**: Complements Raskin: Raskin supplies emotional stakes (elements 1–3), Dunford supplies decision logic — strong decks use Raskin's setup with Dunford's follow-through.

#### D9. Challenger Sale commercial-teaching choreography
- **Structure**: (1) Warmer — prove you understand their world (peer data, no product) → (2) Reframe — one counterintuitive insight challenging a belief they actually hold → (3) Rational Drowning — data quantifying the hidden cost of the status quo → (4) Emotional Impact — a story that puts THIS customer inside the problem → (5) A New Way — required capabilities, still solution-agnostic → (6) Your Solution — only now.
- **When to use**: Complex B2B sales to status-quo-biased or skeptical buyers; competitive displacement; buyers who don't yet believe they have a problem.
- **Slide mapping**: 1–2 their industry/numbers/peers; 3 the one surprising claim (the pivot slide of the whole deck); 4–6 cost-of-inaction data; 7 a named, relatable pain story; 8–9 capability checklist; final 2–3 your solution mapped to that checklist + proof + ask.
- **Pitfalls**: The product appears in the LAST sixth — the discipline most sales decks fail. A reframe of a strawman kills credibility. Order matters: numbers create concern (Rational Drowning) BEFORE story makes it personal.
- **Relatives**: The hostile/indifferent-prospect variant of delayed-thesis ordering (F4) applied to sales; note it inverts H7's story-then-stats because its job is belief-change, not empathy.
